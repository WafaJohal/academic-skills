# Writing Patterns from Approved Ethics Applications

These patterns are distilled from approved applications in a computing and information systems
department. Adapt tone and content to the specific research project.

---

## General Tone and Style

- **Plain language** — write as if explaining to an intelligent non-specialist reviewer
- **Active voice** — "We will collect..." not "Data will be collected..."
- **Structured but readable** — use short paragraphs or labelled sub-sections within free text fields
- **Specific** — name tools, platforms, timelines; avoid vague statements like "appropriate measures"
- **Complete** — the committee should not need to ask follow-up questions; anticipate their concerns

---

## Project Summary (Plain Language) — Structure Pattern

```
AIMS
[2–3 sentences explaining the research gap and what the project will do about it]

METHODS
[Describe phase by phase if multi-phase, or method by method]
Phase 1 – [Name]: [what happens, what is collected]
Phase 2 – [Name]: [what happens, what is collected]
...

PARTICIPANT GROUPS
[Who they are, where from, how recruited, what they'll do, how they consent]

RISKS
[List risks with brief mitigations for each]

BENEFITS
[Academic/societal benefits; participant benefits if any]
```

---

## Background — Writing Pattern

Open by situating the research in the current landscape:
> "As [technology/field X] becomes increasingly [characteristic], [problem or gap] has emerged..."

State the gap clearly:
> "Currently, there is no [validated tool/framework/study] that [addresses X]."

State what this project does:
> "This project addresses this critical gap by [developing/investigating/validating] [X]."

Include 3–5 key references in parenthetical citations (not full bibliography).
Keep to 3–4 paragraphs max.

---

## Risk Description — Writing Pattern

Structure each risk as a numbered item with name + explanation:

```
(1) A minor risk of [risk name]:
[One paragraph explaining: what the risk is, how it could arise in this specific research, and potential consequences]

(2) A minor risk due to [risk name]:
[Same pattern]
```

**Common risks and their standard mitigations:**

| Risk | Standard mitigation language |
|------|------------------------------|
| Privacy breach | "All data will be stored securely on institution-approved systems, accessible only to named researchers. Participants will be assigned pseudonyms." |
| Confidential business information | "Participants will be reminded in the PLS not to share confidential information and may withdraw any information post-study." |
| Coercion (employer-paid) | "We believe this is low risk as we will not work with vulnerable groups and most participants are stakeholders who agreed to the project. The PLS outlines clear withdrawal steps." |
| Minimal/no risk (online surveys) | "Risks are minimal. Participants will only be asked to complete anonymous online surveys about [topic]. There are no tasks involving personal, medical, financial, or sensitive information." |
| Physical risk (XR, site visits) | "All [XR/site] activities will be conducted in a controlled environment with the research team present. Safety protocols will be briefed in advance and first aid is available." |
| Survey fatigue | "Surveys are designed to be short (~15–30 minutes) to reduce fatigue. Participants may skip questions or withdraw at any time." |

## Risk Management — Writing Pattern

```
To minimise risks, the following measures are in place:

([Risk number]) [Risk name]:
1. [Measure 1]
2. [Measure 2]
3. [Measure 3]
```

## Benefits — Writing Pattern

Structure as:
1. **Societal/community benefits** — the "so what" for the broader world
2. **Research/academic benefits** — contribution to knowledge, filling a gap
3. **Participant benefits** — direct benefits to those in the study (often minimal; acknowledge this)

> "Although there are no direct material benefits to participants, some may gain [X]. Participants will also contribute to [Y] that may ultimately lead to [Z] for society."

## Consent Justification — Writing Patterns by Type

**Implied consent (online surveys):**
> "Consent will be implied by participants choosing to proceed past the information screen and begin the survey. This method is appropriate for low-risk, online research involving adult participants who are competent to provide consent and are not in a dependent relationship with the researchers."

**Written consent (interviews/workshops):**
> "Written consent will be sought from all participants prior to each activity. Participants will receive the Plain Language Statement via email and sign the Consent Form using a digital signature tool (e.g., DocuSign) before attending [interviews/workshops]. This ensures participants have adequate time to read, understand, and make an informed decision about their participation."

**On-site consent (site visits):**
> "Participants will be provided with a Plain Language Statement on arrival at the site. They will be asked to read and sign the Consent Form in person, and confirm their consent to any audio recording or photography, before the activity begins."

## Data Storage — Writing Pattern

**Digital data (standard HCI/IS research):**
> "All data will be collected and stored securely on [Your Institution]'s institution-approved cloud storage, accessible only to the named researchers on this project. [If using Qualtrics:] Online survey responses will be collected via the institution's Qualtrics instance, hosted on institution-managed servers. [If using Dovetail:] Qualitative data will be analysed using Dovetail, an Australian-based research tool that stores data on Amazon AWS servers within Australia, encrypted at rest using AES-256 and in transit using TLS 1.3. All data will be downloaded to institutional storage and deleted from Dovetail once analysis is complete. Data will be retained for five years after completion of the research activity, in accordance with the institution's Records & Disposal Authority, after which it will be securely deleted."

## Trans-border Data Flow — Writing Pattern

**When international participants recruited via Prolific:**
> "This project involves trans-border data flow in two respects.
> *International Participants:* Some participants will be recruited via Prolific (Stages X–X), an international online research platform. However, all survey responses will be collected through Qualtrics, hosted on secure servers managed by [Your Institution] within Australia. No personal identifiers or sensitive personal data will be collected that could identify participants across borders. Only anonymous and/or aggregated data will be downloaded for analysis.
> *International Research Team Member:* [If applicable] One member of the research team is based in [country] and may access anonymised research data for analysis. Only anonymous datasets will be shared with the overseas researcher. No identifiable or sensitive personal data will leave Australia. Data transfer will be conducted via secure, institution-approved systems."

## Personnel Role Descriptions — Patterns

**Project Supervisor:**
> "[Name] is the Project Supervisor and primary investigator. [She/He/They] is responsible for the overall design, management, and ethical conduct of the project. [She/He/They] provides weekly feedback to the research team, oversees data collection and analysis, and ensures compliance with the approved ethics protocol and the National Statement."

**PhD student:**
> "[Name] is a final-year doctoral researcher responsible for the study design, data collection, analysis, and report writing. [She/He/They] has previously conducted [describe prior relevant experience] and has completed [your institution's mandatory research integrity training]."

**External collaborator:**
> "[Name] is [title] at [institution] and serves as a subject matter expert in [area]. [She/He/They] will contribute to [study design/item development/analysis] and will access only de-identified, anonymised datasets in accordance with [Your Institution]'s data security protocols."

## Overseas Participant Section (P8) — Pattern

**Cultural values:**
> "This project involves anonymous online surveys on general perceptions of [topic], avoiding culturally sensitive topics. Language will be clear and culturally neutral, and participation is voluntary with the option to skip questions. [If EU:] For EU participants, GDPR compliance will be ensured. Researchers will remain respectful of cultural differences in all interactions."

**Power relations:**
> "This research involves anonymous online surveys about [topic], without addressing personal, political, or culturally sensitive topics. The survey avoids content that could reinforce power imbalances or social divisions. Participation is entirely voluntary and no identifying information will be collected, helping to minimise risks related to local power dynamics or inequalities."

## Project Management — Pattern

> "[Supervisor name] will oversee the project and is responsible for ensuring compliance with the approved protocol and all applicable legislation and guidelines. [Student/Fellow name] will conduct day-to-day research activities including [recruitment/data collection/analysis] under [Supervisor's] supervision. The team meets [weekly/fortnightly] to review progress and ensure alignment with approved protocols. [Supervisor name] must review and approve all participant-facing materials and data collection instruments before use."

## Risk/Benefit Justification — Pattern

> "This project poses [minimal/minor] risk to participants. The research involves [brief description — e.g., completing anonymous online surveys about perceptions of AI systems]. The most likely burden is [mild inconvenience due to time commitment of approximately X minutes / etc.].
>
> In contrast, the potential benefits of the research are significant: [list 2–3 specific benefits]. These benefits substantially outweigh the minor and manageable risks identified above, and the research team has taken appropriate measures to minimise all identified risks."
